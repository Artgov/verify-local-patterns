This page is for people who work in local authorities. It outlines a common pattern for providing resident's parking permits services effectively online, based on user needs. Read more about service patterns.

Example service

As you read the service pattern description, explore the example council service.

What is the user need?

Residents need a place to park their car near their home. Parking permit services make sure there are parking spaces available for residents. They make it illegal to park in some areas without a permit.

Understand other user needs for this service

What does your service need from users to deliver this service?

Depending on your local authority's needs, users may be asked to provide:

  • the postcode of the area where they want to park
  • proof they live in the area
  • the registration number of the vehicle to be permitted
  • payment (to support the cost of the service – may vary from council to council and within the service, for example depending on parking zone)

What is the user journey?

{% include 'includes/pp-user-journey-diagram.html' %}

These are the steps users should go through.

1. Awareness

Users need to be made aware the need for a permit and also how to apply for that permit so that they are able to recieve the permit before they need to park their car. This could be through:

  • direct mail to residents in areas affected by new parking schemes
  • messaging to users new users of other council services in the area
  • information provided to new home owners or tenants in parking areas
  • street signage

Your service needs to make sure correct information is being provided through all appropriate channels within your control and that this information addresses both the need for a permit and how to apply.

2. Finding the right council

When the user knows they need a permit, they need to get to the right council's website to apply. Users might start by typing 'parking permit' into a search engine, or going to the website of the council where they think they need a permit.

Search engine

The GOV.UK parking permits page is the top result in most search engines. It should send users to the right council's website when they enter their postcode. Your service needs to make sure the postcodes it provides services for are linked to the right page on their website and email any corrections to local-direct-admin@digital.cabinet-office.gov.uk.

Council website

Users may also go straight to the website for the council they think they need to get a permit from. This makes it very important for your service's webpage to clearly state which area your parking permit service covers – as the user may be in the wrong place.

3. Parking start page

Some users may not go through the two steps above. They may arrive on your council's website with a need to park, but no knowledge that they need a permit. So it's important to explain they may need a permit.

4. Finding the right parking zone

The service asks for the postcode of where the user wants to park, so that it can:

  • identify which parking zone the user will need a permit for
  • check availability of permits in the parking zone and handle the application accordingly
  • check the user is a resident within the parking zone

These checks take place early in the user journey to ensure the application is for the correct permit.

6. Resident's start page

Now that the zone and permit type are known, this page should communicate:

  • permit eligibility requirements
  • how much the permit will cost
  • how long the permit is valid for
  • what needs to be provided to complete the application
  • where the permit will allow them to park
  • when the permit will allow them to park
  • how long the process usually takes
  • what to do if they need assistance with the application

8. Confirm address details

GOV.UK Verify will send the council the user's address, as supplied by the user's chosen identity provider. When the user returns from the GOV.UK Verify the service should ask them to confirm if this is their current address.

Explore the confirm name and address example council page

If the user says the address supplied through GOV.UK Verify is not correct, they are likely to be a new resident whose address has not been updated with their identity provider. The user should then be asked to enter their current address and be warned that they must not enter false information.

Explore the enter current address example council page

If the parking permit your council issues is physical, your service can gain extra confidence that the applicant's address is current by mailing the permit to the address they've confirmed in their online application. Mention you'll be using the address for this purpose on the confirmation page.

If the permit your council issues is digital and your serice wants extra confidence of eligibility, you can issue a short-term permit, for example for 3 months. After this time, the user's identity provider should have the new address and the user can re-apply using GOV.UK Verify.

9. Address check for validity and permit numbers

a. Does the user live in the area?

At this point your service should check if the user lives in the eligible area, and notify them if they are not eligible. This can be done by checking the user's address against your council's database of parking permit zones.

Explore the not eligible due to address example council page

b. Does the user or the user's address already have a permit?

Your council may limit the number of people who can get a permit, or number of permits allowed per address. At the point where the online application checks address eligibility, it should also check if any permits are currently assigned to the person or their address and issue an ineligible message if applicable.

10. Enter vehicle registration

Some councils require the permit to be associated with a specific vehicle.

If your service requires this for enforcement purposes, the service needs to specify that this is why you need the data.

In some councils, the permit price varies depending on the vehicle type, emissions, make or other conditions.

If your council requires this information, your service can look it up using DVLA's vehicle details API. In this case, the service should explain to users that their registration number will be used for this purpose as well.

Some councils require users to provide either their V5C number, or a scan or the V5C form. If your council requires this information the service should explain to users why this information is required and how it will be used.

11. Choosing permit length

This page should only be displayed if the user has a choice of permit at this point. Generally local councils give users a choice of 6 month or 12 month permits.

12. Paying for a permit

Permit fees will vary based on fee calculation rules determined by each individual council. In some cases the permit may be issued free of charge, for example, if the resident is the first member of the household to apply.

Explore accepting payments example council pages

Access related user research

Your service can accept payment via a number of channels, including checks, bank transfers or online payments. Councils can find a supplier on the Digital Marketplace.

In the future, your service will be able to accept payments using GOV.UK Pay.

13. Receive permit confirmation

After payment, the user needs to receive a confirmation of payment and an acknowledgement that their application has been successful. They should also receive information about how the permit will be enforced (see step 8).

If the payment cannot be accepted or if the application fails, for example it times out, the user should be shown an on-screen message that specifies the next steps to take to continue with the application.

Your service should ask the user how they would like to receive this confirmation of payment, offering the choice of email, letter or text. See the example page on how to do this.

In the future, your service will be able to send this confirmation using GOV.UK Notify.

14. Contacting users

Users need to know that they will be informed if anything means their application will not be completed.

Users also need to know when they should renew their permit.

Allow your users to select their prefered contact method and only contact them in this way in future.

Explore the preferred contact details example council page

Access related user research

15. Checking permits

It's better for users and cheaper for government if permits are issued electronically. Wardens check electronic permits by entering registration numbers into a handheld device linked to a database.

If your service hasn't yet moved over to an electronic permit system, it should have a plan in place to move over.

Until then, your service should mention in the physical permit payment receipt

  • how long the user will need to wait for a permit to be posted to them
  • if the user can pick up their permit from a council office to get it faster and where that is
  • if the user can print a temporary permit themselves while they wait for a permanent one
  • this information should also be communicated on the council's start page at the beginning of the user journey
  • advice on how customers can park without their permit until they receive it

This information should also be communicated on your service's start page at the beginning of the user journey.

Temporary permits should clearly show their expiry date so wardens and users can check them easily.

16. Renewals

Your service should remind users when it's time for them to renew their permits, using the same contact method used for the payment receipt.
